### Primary Framework: The Geders-Perry Temporal Line

Roberts will anchor his analysis in **temporal distinctions** rather than content-based analysis. The key textual hooks:

1. **Perry's explicit language**: "*During a lengthier recess...it is the defendant's right to unrestricted access to his lawyer*" (488 U.S. at 284). 2. **The 24-hour duration**: Roberts will likely note this exceeds even the 17-hour recess in *Geders*. The temporal magnitude matters because overnight recesses are "often times of intensive work, with tactical decisions to be made and strategies to be reviewed."

3. **Structural constitutional principle**: Roberts respects bright-line rules that preserve constitutional rights while giving clear guidance to trial courts. The *Geders*-*Perry* temporal distinction serves this function—15 minutes permits restrictions, overnight does not.

### Why Roberts Won't Adopt Texas's Content-Based Distinction

Roberts will reject Texas's attempt to distinguish between "testimony discussions" and "strategy discussions" for three reasons grounded in his judicial philosophy:

1. **Administrability**: Roberts is deeply concerned with creating workable rules for trial courts. The content-based distinction Texas proposes is **inherently unworkable**—as oral argument revealed, warning a defendant about excluded evidence "necessarily references the testimony itself." Roberts won't create a rule that requires trial judges to parse attorney-client communications.

2. **Textual clarity**: *Perry* already resolved this. When it said discussions during overnight recesses will "inevitably include some consideration of the defendant's ongoing testimony," it treated testimony discussion as **part of** the protected consultation right, not an exception to it.

3. **Precedential stability**: Roberts authored *Arizona v. United States* (2012), emphasizing that federal preemption doctrine requires clear lines. He'll apply similar reasoning here: *Geders* established a clear temporal rule 47 years ago. Overruling or substantially limiting it based on content distinctions would destabilize criminal procedure without clear textual warrant.

### The Institutional Constraints Framework

Roberts will likely write or join narrowly, emphasizing what the Court is **not** deciding:

- Not addressing whether *all* restrictions during overnight recesses are categorically prohibited
- Not opining on whether different factual circumstances (e.g., documented witness tampering) might permit some limitations
- Not reaching the constitutional merits of whether the Sixth Amendment independently requires this result

This is classic Roberts: decide the case on the narrowest ground (*Geders* controls) while preserving institutional capital and leaving flexibility for future cases.

## 3. DOCTRINAL REASONING

### Primary Framework: Textual Originalism in Criminal Procedure

Thomas will anchor his reasoning in the **original public meaning of the Sixth Amendment's text**: "In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right...to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence."

**Key Textual Hook**: The word **"Assistance"** is doing critical work. At the Founding, "assistance" meant active help, aid, and support—not mere physical presence. A lawyer who cannot speak to his client about the very testimony the client is giving provides no meaningful "Assistance." Thomas is highly likely to emphasize that the Founders would have understood "Assistance of Counsel" to include the ability to consult about ongoing testimony during natural breaks in proceedings.

**Historical Practice**: Thomas will likely examine 18th-century criminal trial practices. While trials were faster then, overnight recesses did occur, and there's no historical evidence of courts prohibiting attorney-client consultation during such breaks. The absence of this practice at the Founding strongly suggests the restriction here violates original meaning.

### Secondary Framework: Rejection of Judicial Interest-Balancing

Thomas consistently rejects balancing tests that weigh constitutional rights against governmental interests. In *Crawford v. Washington* (confrontation), *Apprendi* (jury trial), and numerous other criminal procedure cases, Thomas has insisted that enumerated rights are categorical, not subject to cost-benefit analysis.

The government's arguments about preventing "coaching" or maintaining "trial purity" are precisely the kind of consequentialist policy rationales Thomas dismisses when a clear textual right exists. If the Sixth Amendment protects consultation, the government cannot override it by pointing to administrative convenience or speculation about witness credibility.

### Distinguishing Civil vs. Criminal Procedure

Thomas draws sharp lines between civil and criminal constitutional protections. His willingness to give trial courts broad discretion in civil litigation (as evidenced in some of his opinions on discovery or case management) does **not** carry over to criminal cases where the Sixth Amendment explicitly guarantees rights to the accused. This is a Sixth Amendment criminal procedure case, placing it in Thomas's most textualist, least deferential category.

### The Geders-Perry Framework

While Thomas wasn't on the Court for *Geders* (1976) or *Perry* (1989), their holdings fit his jurisprudence:

- *Geders* held that completely prohibiting attorney-client consultation during a 17-hour overnight recess violated the Sixth Amendment
- *Perry* distinguished brief (15-minute) recesses, upholding restrictions there
- Both opinions rest on the functional reality that the right to counsel means actual assistance, not just symbolic presence

Thomas will likely treat *Geders* as correctly decided because it implements the Sixth Amendment's original meaning. The 24-hour restriction here is even more problematic than *Geders*—it's **longer** than the 17-hour recess and applies to a defendant facing 60 years.

Critically, *Perry* itself reaffirmed that during overnight recesses, defendants have "unrestricted access" to counsel, and discussions will "inevitably include some consideration of the defendant's ongoing testimony." Thomas will read this as consistent with the Sixth Amendment's original meaning and apply it straightforwardly here.

### Key Textual Hook
The phrase "Assistance of Counsel **for his defence**" (emphasis Alito would add) suggests counsel's role is to provide effective defense representation, not to guarantee unlimited communication on all subjects at all times. The question becomes: Does preventing discussion of *ongoing testimony* during a recess deny Villarreal the "assistance" needed "for his defence"?

### Distinguishing Geders v. United States
While *Geders* (1976) held that a **complete ban** on all attorney-client communication during an overnight recess violated the Sixth Amendment, Alito would distinguish this case on three grounds:

1. **Partial vs. Total Restriction**: The trial court here allowed discussion of "other trial-related matters" while prohibiting only discussion of Villarreal's ongoing testimony. This is a **content-based limitation**, not a wholesale deprivation of counsel. Alito would cite the principle from numerous First Amendment cases that content-based restrictions, while subject to scrutiny, are not *per se* unconstitutional. The question is whether this particular content restriction denies effective assistance.

2. **Preventing Witness Coaching vs. Strategic Counseling**: Alito would embrace the State's distinction between legitimate trial strategy discussions (protected) and improper mid-testimony coaching (regulable). He would characterize *Geders* as establishing a floor—states cannot impose *total communication bans*—but not a ceiling that prevents all content-based limitations. The trial judge here exercised traditional judicial authority to prevent witness tampering and ensure testimonial integrity.

3. **State Procedural Autonomy**: Drawing from his federalism jurisprudence (particularly his dissent in *Torres v. Texas Dept. of Public Safety* and his majority in *Oklahoma v. Castro-Huerta*), Alito would emphasize that states retain substantial authority to regulate trial procedures. The Sixth Amendment establishes minimum guarantees but does not micromanage state trial practices.

### Precedential Support from Case Summaries

**Perry v. Leeke (1989)** provides Alito's strongest precedential hook. *Perry* upheld a 15-minute communication ban during a brief recess, and critically, the Court stated that during overnight recesses, defendants have access "for advice on a variety of **trial-related matters**" and that discussions will "**inevitably include some consideration**" of ongoing testimony. Alito would read this language as Justice White's dissent interpreted it in *Jennings v. Rodriguez*—the protected right is to strategic counseling, with testimony discussion merely *incidental*, not independently protected. The State may reasonably conclude that preventing *direct* testimony discussion while permitting all other strategic discussions strikes the constitutional balance.

**From the oral argument analysis** (Parts III-C and IV), Alito would likely focus on Justice Thomas's questioning about "anything you couldn't discuss involving his testimony before the jury" (Tr. 6-9), suggesting the restriction tracks existing ethical boundaries. Alito might adopt Justice Gorsuch's historical analysis (Tr. 20-25) about longstanding traditions limiting attorney-witness interaction once testimony begins.

### The Coaching vs. Management Distinction
Alito would find this distinction both administrable and constitutionally sufficient:

- **Impermissible coaching**: Altering testimony substance, inducing false testimony, or managing witness performance mid-testimony
- **Permissible counseling**: Advising on evidentiary compliance, trial strategy, plea negotiations—all of which the trial court explicitly permitted

Alito would reject Villarreal's hypotheticals (warning about excluded evidence, etc.) as either:
(a) Permissible under the trial court's order (since they concern *evidentiary rules* rather than *testimony content*), or
(b) Severable problems that don't require invalidating the entire restriction

Alito would resist creating a constitutional rule requiring courts to permit mid-testimony consultation on testimony itself. This would represent judicial micromanagement of trial procedures better left to state courts, legislatures, and rules of professional conduct. The Constitution establishes broad guarantees; states implement them through procedural rules. Absent clear Supreme Court precedent mandating unrestricted testimony discussions, Alito would defer to the trial court's reasonable judgment.

2. **Alternative Remedies**: Citing *Geders*, Gorsuch will note other mechanisms address coaching (cross-examination, perjury prosecutions, ethical sanctions) "short of putting a barrier between client and counsel."

3. **Presumption of Ethical Conduct**: In *McDonnell v. United States* (2016), Gorsuch (in his Tenth Circuit opinion) emphasized that courts should not presume attorneys will act unethically. The restriction here inverts this presumption.

### Most Influential Precedent: *Perry v. Leeke*

Drawing on legal ethics rules (Model Rule 3.3) and the attorney's professional obligations, she'll argue courts already distinguish permissible strategic advice from impermissible subornation of perjury through:
- Cross-examination (exposing coached testimony)
- Sanctions for attorney misconduct
- The attorney's independent ethical duty to prevent perjury

The examples from oral argument (warning about excluded evidence, discussing plea negotiations based on testimony's reception, preparing for cross-examination) all fall on the "counseling" side and are constitutionally essential.

**3. Structural Error Analysis**
Jackson will characterize this as structural error under *United States v. Cronic*, not requiring Strickland prejudice analysis. When the right to consult counsel is completely foreclosed during a critical stage (the overnight break when strategic decisions must be made), prejudice is presumed. This aligns with her approach in *Hewitt v. United States* (dissent), where she emphasized that certain constitutional violations are so fundamental they don't require showing specific harm.

### Critical Textual Hooks:
- **"Assistance"** in Sixth Amendment requires functional help, not decorative presence
- **"Shall have"** is mandatory language—no implied exceptions for overnight recesses
- **"For his defence"** encompasses all aspects of mounting a defense, including mid-trial strategic adjustments

### Precedents Most Influential for Jackson:

**Strongly Supportive:**
- *Geders v. United States* (overnight recess protection)
- *Perry v. Leeke* (reaffirming Geders for overnight recesses)
- *Powell v. Alabama* (functional effectiveness requirement)
- *United States v. Cronic* (structural error when counsel effectively absent at critical stage)

**Distinguishable:**
Jackson will distinguish *Weatherford v. Bursey* (where government agent sat in on attorney-client meetings) because there the defendant **had** attorney consultation—the question was whether third-party presence violated rights. Here, consultation is **completely foreclosed**.

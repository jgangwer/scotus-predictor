## 3. DOCTRINAL REASONING

### Primary Framework: First Amendment Strict Scrutiny
Roberts will apply the exacting scrutiny framework he established in **McCutcheon v. FEC** (2014), which he authored. Three textual/doctrinal hooks drive this:

**The Anti-Circumvention Principle's Limits**: In *McCutcheon*, Roberts wrote that preventing circumvention of base contribution limits is a valid interest, but *only* when the chain of events is "plausible" and doesn't rest on "prophylaxis-upon-prophylaxis." The key passage: "The Government may not... seek to limit the appearance of mere influence or access" and must show that limits prevent "quid pro quo corruption or its appearance."

Applied here, Roberts will find the coordination limits fail because:
- The government hasn't shown actual circumvention risk from party coordination (no examples from 28 states allowing it)
- This is a "prophylaxis on top of a prophylaxis"—limiting coordination to prevent circumvention of contribution limits already in place
- Super PACs create identical "circumvention" opportunities, making the party restriction under-inclusive

**The Narrow Tailoring Requirement**: Roberts's *McCutcheon* opinion emphasized: "When the Government restricts speech, the Government bears the burden of proving the constitutionality of its actions." He required the government to show coordination limits are "closely drawn" to prevent *actual* quid pro quo corruption. The absence of corruption evidence from permissive states will be dispositive for Roberts.

**Changed Circumstances Since *Colorado II***: Roberts will likely distinguish rather than overrule *Colorado II* (as suggested in oral argument where neither side fully defended it). The 2014 statutory amendments creating exceptions to party contribution limits (convention funding, headquarters, legal proceedings) undermine the circumvention rationale Roberts found insufficient in *McCutcheon*. If Congress allows unlimited party coordination for these purposes, the blanket prohibition can't survive.

### Secondary Framework: Constitutional Avoidance (Potential Off-Ramp)
Roberts might invoke **Jennings v. Rodriguez** (2018) principles about avoiding constitutional questions by strict statutory construction. However, this seems unlikely here because:
- The statutory text is unambiguous about limiting coordination
- Roberts has shown willingness to reach First Amendment questions in campaign finance cases when the government's justification is weak
- The alternative—remand for as-applied challenges case-by-case—contradicts his efficiency preferences seen in **King v. Burwell**

### Least Likely But Possible: Standing/Mootness Exit
Roberts could potentially join Thomas's §1252(b)(9) jurisdiction-stripping analysis, but this contradicts his typical preference for reaching merits in politically significant cases. His **NFIB v. Sebelius** approach (finding a way to reach and decide) suggests he'll address the constitutional question directly.

## 3. DOCTRINAL REASONING

### Primary Framework: Exacting Scrutiny for Campaign Finance Restrictions

Alito will apply the framework from **McCutcheon v. FEC** (2014), which he joined and which represents his most developed campaign finance reasoning. The key textual hooks:

**Constitutional Text**: First Amendment's "Congress shall make no law...abridging the freedom of speech" receives full-strength protection for political speech—the "core" of First Amendment concern.

**McCutcheon's Three-Part Test**:
1. Does the restriction burden protected political speech? (Yes—parties cannot coordinate messaging)
2. Does it serve a compelling governmental interest? (Only *quid pro quo* corruption, not "influence")
3. Is it narrowly tailored? (Must be the *least restrictive means*)

### Critical Doctrinal Moves

**A. Rejection of "Prophylaxis-Upon-Prophylaxis"**

From *McCutcheon* (which Alito joined): Congress cannot create "prophylactic" measures to prevent circumvention of other prophylactic measures. Here:
- Base contribution limits = prophylactic measure #1
- Coordinated expenditure limits = prophylactic measure #2 (preventing circumvention of #1)
- This is the precise "prophylaxis-upon-prophylaxis" that *McCutcheon* rejected

Alito likely quotes *McCutcheon*'s language: "When the Government restricts speech, the Government bears the burden of proving the constitutionality of its actions...The Government may not...restrict one kind of speech in order to prevent manipulation of another kind."

**B. Colorado II Is Distinguishable or Wrongly Decided**

Three textual/doctrinal routes Alito could take:

**Route 1 (Most Likely)**: *As-Applied Distinction*
- *Colorado II* addressed *facial* challenge (p. 456 n.17 explicitly reserved as-applied challenges)
- Here, NRSC presents specific evidence: 28 states allow unlimited coordination without documented corruption
- Justice Scalia in *Colorado II* dissent (which Alito later embraced jurisprudentially) argued the Court's speculation about circumvention lacked empirical support
- Alito frames this as "*Colorado II* on its own terms reserved the as-applied question we decide today"

**Route 2 (Moderate Likelihood)**: Doctrinal Evolution
- *Cruz* (2022) clarified that **only** *quid pro quo* corruption justifies restrictions
- *Colorado II*'s acceptance of "undue influence" and "appearance of corruption" (533 U.S. at 441) is no longer good law post-*Cruz*
- 2014 statutory amendments (three exceptions to party coordination limits) undermine the anti-circumvention rationale

**Route 3 (Lower Likelihood but Possible)**: Outright Overruling
- Alito joins Thomas's view that *stare decisis* is weakest for First Amendment cases restricting core political speech
- *Colorado II* rests on empirical predictions ("beyond serious doubt" of circumvention) that 23 years of state experience have disproven

### C. The "Coordination = Contribution" Fiction

The statutory equation in § 30116(d)—treating coordinated expenditures as "contributions"—is constitutionally problematic under *Buckley*'s framework:

- *Buckley* distinguished **contributions** (subject to limits, less speech value) from **expenditures** (cannot be limited, core speech)
- Alito will emphasize that **coordination doesn't change the speech's nature**—it remains the party's own expenditure speaking its own message
- The fact that coordination makes speech *more effective* (the very purpose of campaigns) cannot justify restrictions without proving *quid pro quo* corruption

**Textual hook from *Buckley***: "A restriction on the amount of money a person or group can spend on political communication during a campaign necessarily reduces the quantity of expression by restricting the number of issues discussed, the depth of their exploration, and the size of the audience reached."

### Key Textual Hook: "Coordination" as the Functional Equivalent of Direct Contribution

Sotomayor will emphasize that **52 U.S.C. §30116(d)**'s treatment of coordinated expenditures as "contributions" reflects Congress's reasonable judgment that coordination eliminates the independence that justifies different treatment of expenditures versus contributions. ### Precedential Foundation: Reaffirming Colorado II

Unlike the majority's likely path of distinguishing or implicitly overruling **FEC v. Colorado Republican Federal Campaign Committee (Colorado II)**, Sotomayor will embrace it. 1. Parties can serve as conduits for circumventing contribution limits
2. The "closely drawn" standard permits prophylactic measures
3. Preventing "undue influence on an officeholder's judgment" is a valid government interest

Sotomayor will reject the petitioners' argument that **McCutcheon v. FEC** (2014) and **FEC v. Cruz** (2022) narrowed permissible government interests to only direct quid pro quo corruption. She will read these cases as refining—not eliminating—the broader anti-corruption rationale that animated **Buckley v. Valeo** (1976).

### Rejecting the Major Questions/Clear Statement Approach

The petitioners argue (and the conservative majority will likely agree) that such a significant restriction on political speech requires crystal-clear statutory authorization and survives only if essential to preventing proven corruption. Sotomayor will reject this framing for three reasons:

1. **Deference to Congressional Fact-Finding**: Congress compiled extensive evidence of party committees serving as circumvention vehicles. The oral argument shows Sotomayor citing BCRA's legislative findings and the decades of soft money abuses Congress documented. She consistently defers to legislative judgments about systemic risks (**Torres v. Texas Dep't of Public Safety**, **Biden v. Missouri**).

2. **The Constitution Permits—Indeed Requires—Prophylactic Campaign Finance Regulation**: Corruption is difficult to prove precisely because sophisticated actors design arrangements to avoid explicit quid pro quo. Sotomayor will emphasize that **Buckley** itself recognized the "appearance of corruption" as sufficient, and that prophylactic measures are constitutionally necessary.

3. **The Absence of Documented Coordination-Based Corruption Proves the Regulation Works**: This is Sotomayor's likely response to petitioners' argument that no one can identify corruption through coordination. The regulation's success in preventing the harm it targets is evidence of its necessity, not its superfluity.

### Distinguishing Speech from Conduct

Sotomayor will frame coordination limits as regulating the financial *relationship* between parties and candidates—structural regulation of the campaign finance system—rather than direct speech suppression. She made similar moves in **Janus v. AFSCME** (2018) (dissenting from First Amendment expansion into labor relations) and will apply that framework here: the First Amendment does not immunize all monetary transactions from regulation merely because they fund speech.

Kagan will likely anchor her reasoning in the **plain statutory language** of § 30116(d), which treats coordinated expenditures as "contributions" *to the candidate*. The key textual move: coordination transforms what would otherwise be independent spending (protected under *Citizens United*) into the functional equivalent of a direct contribution (regulable under *Buckley*).

**Textual hook from § 30116(d):**
> "Expenditures made by any person in cooperation, consultation, or concert, with... a political party committee... shall be **considered to be a contribution to such candidate**."

This statutory equation—*coordination = contribution*—provides the framework. If coordinated spending *is* a contribution (by statutory definition), then *Colorado II*'s holding that Congress may limit contributions to prevent circumvention of base contribution limits applies directly.

### Why *Colorado II* Remains Controlling

Kagan will likely employ a **two-step stare decisis analysis**:

**Step 1: Has the statutory text changed?** No. Section 30116(d) remains identical. The 2014 amendments created narrow exceptions for specific party functions (conventions, buildings, legal proceedings) but left the general coordination-as-contribution rule untouched. Under Kagan's interpretive approach (see *King v. Burwell*, where she joined Roberts's contextualism), statutory amendments that carve out specific exceptions **reinforce** rather than undermine the general rule—they show Congress knows how to create exceptions when it wants them.

**Step 2: Has the factual predicate collapsed?** Petitioners argue the rise of Super PACs proves coordination limits are now ineffective/unnecessary. Kagan will reject this argument using the logic from her *McCutcheon* dissent: the existence of *other* circumvention routes doesn't mean Congress must allow *this* circumvention route. The fact that unlimited independent spending exists post-*Citizens United* doesn't mean Congress must also permit unlimited *coordinated* spending.

**Key passage from Kagan's *McCutcheon* dissent (joined by Breyer, Sotomayor, Ginsburg):**
> "Taken together, the Republican Party's and the dissent's contentions undermine our [campaign finance] jurisprudence... Where enough money exists in the system, those who want to corrupt will find their way."

This shows Kagan's view: coordination limits serve as **part of an integrated anti-circumvention system**. Removing one component because other vulnerabilities exist would gut the entire structure.

### The 2014 Amendments Don't Create Ambiguity

Petitioners argue the 2014 exceptions for convention/headquarters/legal spending prove Congress abandoned anti-circumvention concerns. Kagan will likely invoke **statutory construction principles** from cases like *Loper Bright* (where she dissented from Chevron overruling but emphasized careful statutory parsing):

The 2014 exceptions are **narrow and functional**:
- **Convention funding** = one-time quadrennial event, publicly visible, not candidate-specific
- **Headquarters buildings** = physical infrastructure, not electioneering
- **Legal proceedings** = defensive expenditures (recounts, compliance), not offensive campaigning

None of these involves **direct electioneering coordination** on messaging and strategy—the core concern in *Colorado II*. Under the canon of *expressio unius est exclusio alterius* (expressing one thing excludes others), Congress's careful crafting of these three exceptions suggests it did **not** intend to except general campaign coordination.

### The State Evidence Cuts Against Petitioners

28 states allow unlimited party coordination, but petitioners identify **zero instances** of demonstrable quid pro quo corruption. Kagan will flip this argument: the **absence of corruption at the state level** doesn't prove the federal prophylaxis is unnecessary—it might prove the **federal limits are working** to prevent a race to the bottom. This parallels her reasoning in *Arizona Free Enterprise Club* (dissent), where she argued that corruption concerns justified Arizona's public financing system even if direct quid pro quo was hard to document.

- Petitioners failed to show the decision is "unworkable" (the Ramos/Janus/Dobbs standard for overruling)
- The 2014 statutory amendments petitioners cite are **narrow exceptions** (convention funding, HQ buildings, legal proceedings) that don't undermine the core anti-circumvention rationale
- Twenty-four years of reliance: the entire post-*Colorado II* regulatory architecture depends on coordination limits

This maps to Jackson's approach in **Trump v. Anderson** (2024), where she joined the majority's narrow statutory ground while writing separately to emphasize democratic stability and avoiding "chaotic state-by-state outcomes." Here, overruling *Colorado II* would create similar chaos in campaign finance regulation.

**C. Rejecting the Major Questions Doctrine Application**

Petitioners will invoke *West Virginia v. EPA* (2022) to argue FEC lacks authority to impose limits with "vast economic and political significance" absent clear congressional statement. Jackson will reject this for three reasons:

1. **Explicit Authorization**: Unlike *West Virginia* (EPA claimed authority under "best system of emission reduction" catchall) or *Biden v. Nebraska* (COVID emergency statute), FECA's text **expressly addresses** party expenditures and coordination. Section 441a(a)(7)(B)(i) explicitly limits party spending "in connection with the general election campaign of a candidate for Federal office."

2. **No Delegation Canon Concerns**: Jackson distinguished *Loper Bright* (2024) by declining to join parts addressing Chevron deference. Here, there's no ambiguity requiring deference—Congress directly regulated the subject matter.

3. **Anti-Circumvention Rationale**: The Court in *FEC v. Colorado Republican Federal Campaign Committee* ("Colorado I," 1996) and *Colorado II* recognized that unlimited party coordination would allow circumvention of contribution limits. Jackson will cite *McConnell*'s approval of BCRA's soft-money ban using identical reasoning.

**D. First Amendment Framework: Intermediate Scrutiny Satisfied**

Jackson will apply *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976)'s framework treating expenditure limits as subject to "exacting scrutiny" but accept that coordinated expenditures are functionally contributions:

- **Governmental Interest**: Preventing circumvention of contribution limits is sufficiently important (*Colorado II*, *McConnell*)
- **Tailoring**: The limits apply only to coordinated spending; parties retain unlimited independent expenditure rights (*Colorado I*)
- **Empirical Basis**: Congress could rationally conclude unlimited coordination creates quid pro quo corruption risks or appearance thereof

She will distinguish *Citizens United* (2010) as addressing independent corporate spending, not coordination between parties and candidates. The *Citizens United* majority preserved *Buckley*'s contribution limits, and coordination regulations are part of that framework.

**E. Functional Democracy Concerns**

Jackson's separate writings emphasize democratic participation and political equality. In **Moore v. Harper** (2023), she joined Roberts's majority rejecting independent state legislature theory partly on democratic legitimacy grounds. In **Allen v. Milligan** (2023), she joined the majority protecting Voting Rights Act enforcement.

Here, she'll likely view unlimited party coordination as:
- Exacerbating wealth inequality in political influence
- Creating "legalized bribery" appearance when parties funnel unlimited donations directly to candidates
- Undermining public confidence in democratic institutions

This maps to Justice Breyer's *Randall v. Sorrell* (2006) concurrence (which Jackson's jurisprudence resembles): campaign finance limits serve "not simply to prevent quid pro quo corruption...but also to promote a government where laws reflect the 'collective views of the people.'"
